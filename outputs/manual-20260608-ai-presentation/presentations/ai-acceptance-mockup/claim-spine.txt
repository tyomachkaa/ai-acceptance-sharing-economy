Thesis:
Users do not reject AI itself; they reject imposed, opaque, non-escalatable AI in high-trust service moments.

Audience:
Online Content Analysis class and instructor.

One-line arc:
Start from the assignment, show how the data and methods meet it, then turn converging text-mining evidence into AI-agent design rules.

Slide list:
1. AI acceptance depends on whether AI is chosen or imposed. Proof: headline metric rail.
2. The project meets the brief, but uses comparison contexts to sharpen the sharing-economy case. Proof: assignment-fit map.
3. Two corpora create two kinds of evidence: open discussion and rated transaction reviews. Proof: data map.
4. The method stack covers the brief and triangulates weak labels against real stars. Proof: pipeline.
5. Vocabulary splits around useful automation versus forced contact friction. Proof: top words.
6. The emotional gap is fundamentally a trust gap. Proof: NRC emotion chart.
7. The network shows the human-exit problem. Proof: word network.
8. LDA turns thousands of comments into interpretable service failure themes. Proof: topic terms.
9. GloVe independently rediscovers the human-versus-automation tension. Proof: neighbour table.
10. Trustpilot validates the Reddit sentiment split well enough for directional inference. Proof: confusion matrix.
11. AI is penalised when it acts as a rental add-on without control or escalation. Proof: role gradient and AI penalty.
12. The design answer is transparent, escapable, and role-matched AI. Proof: recommendation stack and caveats.
